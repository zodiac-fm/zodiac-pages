Design study · 2026-07-01 · reacting to "our colors don't match on the wheel"

Why our four colors clash, and how a family is built.

Our frequency colors were each chosen on their own. Measured together, they don't belong to one set. Here is the honest diagnosis and the fix that world-class palettes use. This is a study to react to, not a locked decision.

The problem

Today: four colors, four different brightnesses.

Each swatch shows its real lightness (L), saturation (C) and hue angle (H) in OKLCH, the space that matches how the eye actually reads color. Notice the lightness numbers: they run from 46% to 76%. That 30-point spread is the "amateur" tell. Orange and aqua jump forward, violet and rose sink back, so the eye never sees them as one family.

Read it in one line: lightness 46 to 76 (a 30-point jump), saturation .132 to .230. Four colors, four different energies. Put them side by side as we do on the app and the brightest one always wins and the set looks like a paint box.
The fix

Governed: same four identities, one brightness, one saturation.

Keep exactly which color means what (violet, rose, amber, aqua), but lock every one to the same lightness and the same saturation. Now they read as siblings from one family. This is what Aesop, Oura, and every serious brand system does. Below at a shared L 62%, C .13.

The other half of the fix

Color is 10% of the page, not the page.

Even a perfect family looks cheap if it floods everything. The rule great brands hold: 60% neutral, 30% one structural brand color, 10% the frequency accents as small marks. The four colors are jewels you place, never paint you pour.
