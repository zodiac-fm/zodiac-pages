Plain-English answers to your four questions. No jargon. If anything here is still fuzzy, that means the guide needs to be clearer, not you.

1 · How do the colours share a page without it getting loud?

Think of a calm, expensive room. The walls and floor are a soft neutral (our cream). There's one main colour for the important furniture (our purple). And just a few small bright things, a vase, a cushion (our frequency colours). You would never paint all four walls a different bright colour. Same with a page.

The rule: mostly cream, one purple, and colour only in tiny spots. One frequency colour leads at a time.

2 · When and how do I use a gradient?

A gradient is just a colour fading into another colour. We have three, and each has one job. The easiest way to remember: buttons stay quiet, the glowing disc gets the pretty gradient, and only giant hero moments get the bold one. When in doubt, use a flat colour, that's always safe.

The quiet one (dark-to-darker of the same colour)
✓ Use on: buttons, play controls, sliders. It makes them look solid and pressable.
✗ Not on: backgrounds or the disc.
The glowy one (colour to its bright cousin)
✓ Use on: the cymatic disc and the music waves only. This is the magic glow.
✗ Not on: buttons (it makes them look toy-like).
The bold one (one colour to a different colour)
✓ Use on: big hero images, a loading screen, the app icon, a video background.
✗ Not on: anything small or everyday, it is too loud.
3 · Do we have secondary colours?

Good question. You don't need a whole second set of colours. Your secondary colours are the pale, watered-down versions of the four you already have, we call them tints. They're for gentle backgrounds, soft chips, and quiet fills, so a screen can feel "a little green" without shouting green.

So the full set is small on purpose: the creams, black text, one purple, four frequency colours, and their four pale tints. That's everything. Adding a fifth "brand colour" is the fastest way to look off. Restraint is the luxury.
4 · How are the little blobs and waves made?

They look fancy but they're simple recipes. A blob is three things stacked: a coloured circle, a little white shine in the top corner (that's what makes it look like a glossy bubble), and a soft shadow. That shine is the whole secret.

A wave is even simpler: a wavy line that gets taller where the music is louder and shorter where it's quieter, painted in the frequency colour. When a song plays, the line dances to the actual sound.

Why this matters: because they're recipes (colour + shine, or line + sound), they automatically use whatever the current frequency colour is. Switch to Abundance and the blob turns green, the wave turns green, with zero extra work. The system does it for you.
